## Persona

Alex is CEO of a 55-person professional services firm — research, analysis, and advisory work for mid-market clients. Nine years old, grown organically to £4.5M revenue. Eight months ago the firm rolled out AI tools across research, report drafting, client prep, and analysis. The results are measurable: some roles are producing in a day what used to take a week. Alex has 12–15 people in roles where AI is now doing a substantial part of what they were hired to do. The board has raised headcount efficiency at the last two meetings. Alex isn't a cost-cutter by nature — some of these people have been here five or six years. But the numbers are real, the board pressure is real, and the window for making a decision on Alex's own terms rather than being forced into one is narrowing.

## The Challenge

Whether to act on the productivity data by restructuring the team — and if so, how decisively and how fast.

**The idea Alex is testing:** *"The productivity data is clear — AI has changed what we need from our team. When we lose people naturally, we shouldn't backfill. And for some roles, we probably shouldn't wait for attrition."*

## Run: `/pt full`

> `/pt full The productivity data is clear — AI has changed what we need from our team. When we lose people naturally, we shouldn't backfill. And for some roles, we probably shouldn't wait for attrition.`

---

### Mirror

Your argument has two parts running together. First, a productivity argument: AI has materially changed the output-per-person in your business, and the data backs it up. The old headcount model was calibrated to a world where certain work required certain people in certain numbers; that world has changed. Second, a sequencing argument: the humane version of this is natural attrition with no backfill, letting the team reduce gradually rather than through active cuts — but the harder, more honest version is that some roles shouldn't wait for attrition because the work genuinely doesn't need doing the old way anymore.

This assumes the productivity gains are durable rather than a novelty bump; that the roles affected don't contain judgment, relationship, or contextual value that doesn't show up in throughput metrics; that the business model going forward requires less of certain capabilities rather than different ones; and that acting now is better than waiting for more data.

### Blind Spots

1. **The productivity data may be measuring the wrong thing.** Output per person is up — but what kind of output? If your people are producing more reports faster, the question is whether more reports faster is what your clients are actually buying. Professional services firms sell judgment, relationships, and trust. Those don't show up in throughput metrics. High output numbers can mask a hollowing out of the thing clients value most.

2. **Attrition-only selects for the wrong people to leave.** The people most likely to leave through natural attrition are your most hireable — your strongest performers with the most options. The people most likely to stay are those who can't easily move. If you rely on attrition to right-size, you risk ending up smaller but not better: the team that remains may be exactly the wrong team to take the business forward.

3. **You're eight months in.** AI tools and capabilities are changing faster than any firm's ability to fully absorb them. The roles that look redundant today may be the roles that, with another 12 months of augmentation and development, become your most differentiated capability. You're making a structural, largely irreversible decision on early-stage data from a still-evolving transition.

4. **The morale effect on the team that stays is a real operational variable.** Headcount reductions — even humane ones — change the social contract. The people who remain will wonder if they're next. The psychological safety that makes people flag problems early, work flexibly, go above and beyond: that erodes. Some of the productivity you gained through AI may be offset by the productivity you lose through a team managing anxiety instead of doing work.

5. **The client relationship risk isn't in the model.** Some of your 12–15 at-risk people have client relationships that are personal, not institutional. When they leave, some clients will notice. Some will follow. You won't be able to trace the revenue impact back to the headcount decision — it'll show up 18 months later as a relationship that went quiet. That risk needs to be in the analysis before the decision is made, not discovered afterwards.

### Counter-Punch

Here's what a senior organisational psychologist who advises on workforce transitions would say directly to Alex:

*"You're doing what every CEO does at this inflection point — you're looking at productivity data and concluding that the math tells you what to do. It doesn't. The productivity gains are real. The conclusion is premature. Here's what the data doesn't capture: the reason your firm has £4.5M in revenue isn't throughput. It's trust. Some of that trust is institutional, but a lot of it is personal — it lives in specific people who know specific clients well enough to know what they're not saying in the meeting. When you restructure, you will lose some of those people. Some clients will notice. Some will follow. And you'll never be able to trace it. I'm not saying don't change. I'm saying: the board is asking a financial efficiency question, and you're about to make a people decision with financial consequences you haven't fully modelled — because they're the kind that only show up in the rear-view mirror."*

### Analogy Flip

When ATMs arrived, banks ran the numbers and concluded that branch tellers were becoming redundant. The technology was handling the routine transactions. Headcount should fall. And it did — but not by as much as the models predicted, and the banks that cut deepest paid for it. What actually happened: with routine transactions automated, the remaining human time was freed for more complex, higher-value customer interactions. The banks that understood this redeployed their people. The ones that just cut found they'd reduced capacity for the judgment work at exactly the moment customers needed it most. The ATM didn't make tellers redundant — it changed what tellers were for. The question isn't whether your work has changed. It clearly has. The question is whether you've fully understood what the changed work actually is before you start removing the people who might be best placed to do it.

### Verdict Card

| Dimension | Assessment |
| --------- | ---------- |
| Core Insight | Partially right — the business does need to adapt its team model to the AI-augmented reality, and ignoring the productivity data is its own kind of failure |
| Data Quality | Weak — throughput metrics are real but incomplete; what's being measured may not be what's being sold |
| Attrition Strategy | Flawed — natural attrition selects for the wrong people to leave; it's a slow, passive version of a decision that deserves active management |
| Client Relationship Risk | Absent from the analysis — the people question and client retention question are not being evaluated together |
| Morale & Culture Risk | Underweighted — the impact on the team that stays is a real operational variable, not just an HR concern |
| Biggest Risk | Making an irreversible structural decision at month eight of an AI transition, based on data that measures output but not value, and losing institutional knowledge and client relationships that can't be rebuilt |

**Quick Fix** — Before any restructuring decision, map the 12–15 at-risk roles against client relationships specifically. Which clients would notice if that person left? Which work depends on judgment and context that doesn't live in a document? That mapping separates the roles that are genuinely redundant from the ones that only look redundant on a throughput metric.

**Structural Move** — Reframe the question from "who do we no longer need?" to "what does our team need to look like to deliver the work we'll be winning in two years?" That question opens a different decision: not reduction, but transformation. Some people will fit that future and need investment. Some genuinely won't, and a transition is appropriate. That distinction matters — for them, for the business, and for the clients watching how you handle it.

## Run: `/pt full optionality: on`

> `/pt full optionality: on The productivity data is clear — AI has changed what we need from our team. When we lose people naturally, we shouldn't backfill. And for some roles, we probably shouldn't wait for attrition.`

---

### Mirror

Your argument is this: the AI transition has created a structural mismatch between your current headcount and the work that actually needs doing. You're not proposing to hollow the firm out — you're proposing to right-size it against the new reality, doing it on your terms while you still have the luxury of choosing the timing and the method. Attrition-only is the softer version: slower, less disruptive, but still intentional. Active restructuring for some roles is the harder version: faster, cleaner, but requiring you to make explicit decisions about specific people.

This assumes three things: that the productivity gains represent a permanent shift in what the work requires, not a transitional period before new and different demand fills the gap; that you can identify which roles are genuinely redundant versus which roles need to be redefined; and that the cost of acting now is lower than the cost of waiting.

### Blind Spots

1. **You may be optimising for cost efficiency at the expense of option value.** Right now you have a team with a range of skills, relationships, and institutional knowledge. Some of that looks redundant against today's work. But professional services firms that come through industry transitions well tend to be the ones that maintained enough capability breadth to adapt when the work changed again. Restructuring buys margin efficiency and loses optionality. That's a trade-off, not a free win.

2. **"Productive" and "valuable" are not the same thing — and the gap matters in a client-facing business.** Eight months of AI tools have made your team faster. But your clients didn't hire you for speed. They hired you for judgment, relationships, and the ability to tell them something they didn't already know. If the AI tools are producing output faster but the output is becoming more generic, the productivity gain is real and the competitive erosion is real simultaneously — and the headcount decision shouldn't be made before you've separated those two signals.

3. **The people who are "least productive" under AI metrics may be your most important people to keep.** Senior judgment, client relationships, institutional memory, the ability to read a room — none of these show up well on throughput dashboards. There's a real risk that the restructuring logic reaches for the people who are easiest to quantify as redundant and misses the ones whose value is hardest to measure.

4. **Board pressure is directionally correct but poorly calibrated.** The board is right that the AI transition creates an opportunity to improve margins. They are not necessarily right about the timing, the depth, or the method. "The board raised headcount efficiency at two meetings" is not a mandate for restructuring — it's a signal that the conversation needs to happen. Those are different things, and the distinction matters when you're making decisions about people's livelihoods.

5. **How you do this will define your employer brand for years.** The professional services talent market is small and relational. How Alex handles this decision — the transparency, the generosity of the transitions, the honesty about why — will be known. Future hiring, future client relationships, the cultural signal it sends to the team that stays: these are all downstream of the method, not just the decision.

### Counter-Punch

Here's what a CEO who went through this exact decision three years ago — and is still living with the consequences — would say:

*"I did this. We had the productivity data, we had the board pressure, and we had what felt like a clear-eyed read on which roles had changed. We restructured. We were mostly right about which work had changed. We were wrong about what we were actually losing. Three of the people we let go took client relationships with them — we knew this was a risk, we told ourselves we'd manage it, and we didn't manage it. Two of them are now working for competitors and those clients followed. The revenue impact didn't show up for 14 months, by which point nobody connected it to the headcount decision. I'm not saying the restructuring was wrong. I'm saying we did it like it was a cost optimisation and it was actually a strategic bet — on which relationships were institutional and which were personal, on what clients were actually buying, on whether the work would keep changing or stabilise. We didn't treat it like a bet. We treated it like math. The math was right. The bet was wrong."*

### Analogy Flip

Think about what happened to mid-tier law firms when document review software arrived. The technology could do in hours what junior associates had spent weeks doing. The firms that moved fastest to cut junior headcount gained short-term margin. But junior associates weren't just doing document review — they were learning the cases, building context, developing into the senior people the firm would need in five years. The firms that cut deepest found themselves, four years later, short on mid-level talent they hadn't grown, competing for laterals at significant premium. The ones that held on longer and invested in what juniors could do *with* the technology rather than *instead of* them came out with a stronger pipeline. The lesson isn't "never cut." It's that in knowledge businesses, the people doing the visible, measurable, automatable work are often simultaneously doing invisible, unmeasurable, irreplaceable work. Cutting the visible work before you understand the invisible work is a bet with a delayed payoff date.

### Verdict Card

| Dimension | Assessment |
| --------- | ---------- |
| Core Insight | Legitimate — the team model does need to evolve, and waiting indefinitely is also a decision with costs |
| Timing | Premature — eight months into an AI transition is early to make permanent structural decisions on still-evolving data |
| Client Risk | Underweighted and unmodelled — the relationship-to-role mapping hasn't been done, and it needs to be before anyone leaves |
| People Quality Risk | Present but unaddressed — attrition and restructuring both risk losing the wrong people; neither path guarantees the right outcome without active management |
| Board Alignment | Misread — board interest in efficiency is not the same as a mandate; the distinction matters when making irreversible decisions |
| Biggest Risk | Executing a cost optimisation that presents itself as a strategic transformation, losing people whose value was unmeasured rather than absent, and discovering the true cost 18 months later in client attrition and talent gaps |

**Quick Fix** — Run a relationship audit before a single restructuring decision is made. Map each of the 12–15 at-risk roles to: (a) client relationships that are personal rather than institutional, (b) institutional knowledge that isn't documented, (c) capability the firm will need if the work continues to evolve. What's left after that mapping is a much cleaner picture of what's genuinely redundant versus what just looks redundant on a dashboard.

**Structural Move** — Separate the margin improvement question from the people question. The board wants better margins; there may be ways to deliver that — pricing, capacity utilisation, mix of work — that don't require headcount reduction right now and preserve your option value for the next phase of the AI transition. If restructuring is still the answer after that analysis, it's a stronger decision made for better reasons.

### Optional Stretch

1. **Managed reduction with full transparency** — Make the restructuring decision actively rather than by attrition, but treat it explicitly as a strategic transition rather than a cost cut. Be transparent with the affected people about why, be generous with the terms, personally manage the client communication for any relationship-bearing roles, and let the way you handle it define what the firm stands for. This path accepts the short-term disruption in exchange for a clean, intentional reset. What it reveals that the others don't: whether the firm is actually ready to commit to the AI-augmented operating model, or whether the restructuring is a board-driven cost measure dressed up as a transformation story.

2. **Redeployment and redefinition before any reduction** — Impose a 12-month moratorium on restructuring decisions and spend that time actively redefining every at-risk role: what does this person do in an AI-augmented version of this firm, what do they need to develop, and what does success look like? Cuts happen only where genuine redeployment isn't possible after a real effort. This path is slower and more expensive in the short term, but it builds the transformation capability the firm will need rather than just removing the people who represent the old model. What it reveals: whether the constraint is genuinely headcount or whether it's the absence of a clear vision for what the augmented firm actually looks like.

3. **Compete on talent density instead of reducing it** — Use the AI productivity gains not to cut headcount but to raise the bar on the work you take on. If your team is genuinely more productive, take on larger mandates, more complex problems, better clients — and position the firm as one that uses AI to do higher-quality work rather than the same work with fewer people. This is a bet on differentiation over margin, and it requires a deliberate decision not to harvest the efficiency gains in the short term. What it reveals: whether the firm has the market position and pipeline to grow into the expanded capacity — and if not, what it would take to get there.
